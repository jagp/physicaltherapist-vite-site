# Thrive Through Midlife

Midlife is a stage to be supported, not endured. The changes that arrive with menopause are real — and the right care keeps you comfortable, confident, and firmly in your own body.

## The changes no one warned you about

Physical therapy for menopause and midlife pelvic health starts by taking your experience seriously. The hormonal shifts of menopause affect the bladder, the pelvic floor, tissue comfort, and intimacy — and too often those changes get waved away as "just menopause." They're not something you simply have to accept.

New bladder urgency. Dryness. Intimacy that has become uncomfortable. These are common midlife experiences with physical, addressable components, and you deserve expert, judgment-free care for all of them.

## A specialist in pelvic health across the lifespan

This chapter deserves a clinician who has seen it all. As a Board-Certified Pelvic Health and Wellness Specialist (PWCS) with more than 45 years of practice, I care for pelvic health across the entire lifespan — including the hormonal transition of menopause — in a private, unhurried, boutique setting where nothing is embarrassing.

As estrogen declines, many women notice a cluster of related changes: vaginal dryness, urinary urgency and frequency, recurrent urinary tract infections, and discomfort with intimacy — together often described as the genitourinary syndrome of menopause (GSM). Much of this can be supported conservatively, through pelvic floor physical therapy and education, without jumping straight to more invasive options. This care also pairs naturally with bone-health work, another priority in these years.

## Common questions

**Can pelvic floor therapy help menopause symptoms?**
Yes — many of menopause's pelvic-health symptoms have physical components that respond to conservative care. Pelvic floor physical therapy and education can help with bladder changes, comfort, and intimacy, often as a first-line approach.

**Why do I have bladder problems during menopause?**
Declining estrogen affects the tissues and muscles that support bladder control, which is why urgency, frequency, and leakage often appear or worsen around menopause. Retraining and supporting the pelvic floor addresses those changes directly.

**Is painful intimacy after menopause treatable?**
Often, yes. Hormonal change can bring dryness and tissue sensitivity that make intimacy uncomfortable — and the pelvic-floor and tissue components of that discomfort can be treated, gently and privately, at your pace.
