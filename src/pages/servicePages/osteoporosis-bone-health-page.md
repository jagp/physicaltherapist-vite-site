# Build Strength That Lasts

The answer to fragile bones is rarely to move less. Done safely, the right exercise loads your bones so they stay strong — and keeps you upright, steady, and confident.

## When a scan makes movement feel risky

Physical therapy for osteoporosis and bone health is about turning that anxious moment — the scan that came back osteoporosis or osteopenia — into a plan. When bone density drops, movement can suddenly feel dangerous, and the instinct is to do less. But the well-supported answer is usually the opposite: load your bones safely, and they respond.

Here, that happens through pelvic-health-informed, **Pilates-based** rehabilitation — safe, progressive loading paired with posture and balance work. It's controlled and supervised, built around where your body is today rather than where a chart says it should be.

## Strength built with care, not heroics

Being Pilates-Rehabilitation certified means I can program safe loading through a whole-body, pelvic-health lens — the kind of controlled, low-impact movement that builds strength without asking your skeleton to take risks it shouldn't.

Safe progressive loading, better posture, and improved balance all support bone density and cut the risk of the falls that turn fragile bones into fractures. That combination matters especially after menopause and for cancer survivors on bone-affecting medication. Just as important is knowing which positions a low-bone-density body should approach with care — so your program is designed around what's safe for *you*. We start where you are, supervised and controlled, no heroics required.

## Common questions

**What exercises are safe if I have osteoporosis?**
Safe, progressive strength training combined with posture and balance work is the foundation — and it's tailored to your bone density. Just as important is guidance on which movements to approach carefully, so your program supports your bones rather than risking them.

**Can physical therapy improve bone density?**
Physical therapy supports bone health by loading bones safely and building the strength, posture, and balance that protect them. That approach helps manage osteoporosis and reduce fracture risk as part of a whole-body plan.

**Is Pilates safe for osteoporosis?**
Pilates-based rehabilitation can be an excellent, low-impact way to build bone-supporting strength — when it's programmed by a certified clinician who adapts the movements for low bone density and steers around the positions that don't suit fragile bones.
